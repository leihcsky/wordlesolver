Connections Hints Today (Spoiler-Free)

Hint 1 Easiest group (yellow band)

Four things a very young child might do or need—sounds and early actions, not jobs, book titles, or cooking verbs.

Watch out: one word also means a medical professional; here it is the feeding sense, not the hospital role.

Hint 2 Second group (green band)

Each word can mean “tamper with the truth” when paired with the right object—think ledgers, numbers, or a story someone tells investigators.

Ignore kitchen, candy, and white-coat senses—only the “change the record dishonestly” verb reading counts for all four.

Hint 3 Third group (blue band)

All four are published works that sit on the same famous author’s YA shelf—one entry is a longer title that contains another shorter title from the set.

Pop-culture memory beats dictionary definitions here; none of these are verbs.

Hint 4 Hardest group (purple band)

Drop a single letter from each word and you land on a sea creature—or something that sounds almost like one. Say them aloud and picture the ocean.

Meaning alone will not help; this is the “say it differently” group saved for last.

How to use these hints

Open hints in color order (yellow → purple). Each card explains the type of link—not the official category headline you see after a correct solve.

  • Yellow band: Solve first—verbs and sounds tied to infancy. If a word feels like a career, it is probably using the wrong definition.
  • Green band: Hunt idioms about falsifying records; “cook” and “doctor” are verbs here, not kitchen or hospital nouns.
  • Blue band: Needs bookshelf memory; cross off anything that is not a novel title.
  • Purple band: Only after three groups are gone—whisper each word minus one letter and listen for aquatic homophones.

Today’s Connections Analysis

Why each band behaves the way it does—still spoiler-free on category names. Use this after the expandable hints to confirm you are not one homograph away from a mistake.

Yellow band Early-life actions

How to identify it: BABBLE, CRY, and TEETHE are unmistakably infant behaviors; the fourth word fits as something babies do with food, not as a hospital job title.

Possible confusion: NURSE tempts a medical reading—today it is the feeding sense. Do not pair it with SURGEON or DOCTOR from other bands.

Green band “Fudge the facts” verbs

How to identify it: ALTER, COOK, DOCTOR, and FUDGE each pair with objects like books, numbers, or evidence when someone is being dishonest—test “cook the books” and “doctor the results” aloud.

Possible confusion: COOK and FUDGE have sweet-food meanings; ignore those. FOREVER is a book title on the board, not part of this verb quartet.

Blue band Shared author shelf

How to identify it: BLUBBER, DEENIE, FOREVER, and SUPERFUDGE are novels—note how one long title embeds a shorter title that also appears as a green-band word, but only as a title fragment here.

Possible confusion: BLUBBER also means whale fat in plain English; the bookshelf sense wins. ALTER and CRY are not titles.

Purple band Letter-drop homophones

How to identify it: FOUNDER, SALON, SURGEON, and TROT share no theme in the dictionary—delete one letter from each and say what you hear; four aquatic names should emerge.

Possible confusion: SURGEON pulls you toward the green band’s “doctor” idea—here it is only the spoken trick. SALON is a hair shop in real life, not the link.

How to Solve Today’s Puzzle Step by Step

  1. Scan for homographs. List alternate meanings for COOK, FUDGE, DOCTOR, NURSE, BLUBBER, and SURGEON before you submit anything—today’s traps are definition switches, not obscure vocabulary.
  2. Lock the yellow band. Once BABBLE, CRY, NURSE (feeding sense), and TEETHE are off the board, you have twelve words and three mistakes left—worth the early confidence.
  3. Test the green quartet as verbs. If all four can complete a phrase about tampering with records (“___ the books,” “___ the numbers”), submit; if one only works as a noun, stop.
  4. Separate titles from verbs. With green gone, the remaining long words should cluster as novels; SUPERFUDGE’s embedded word is a title echo, not the green verb.
  5. Finish with whisper tests. For the last four, drop one letter each and listen—meaning alone will not rescue purple today.

Common Mistakes in Today’s Puzzle

  • Medical mash-up: Grouping NURSE with SURGEON or DOCTOR because they sound clinical—only one of those readings is correct per band.
  • Dessert detour: Treating FUDGE as candy and COOK as a chef while ignoring the ledger-idiom sense that actually unites the green four.
  • Title vs. verb: Pulling FOREVER or SUPERFUDGE into the green band because you spotted the word “fudge” inside a title.
  • Literal purple: Trying to connect SALON to hair or FOUNDER to startups instead of running the one-letter-drop audio test.
  • One-away panic: Submitting a “close enough” foursome when one word still needs a different rule—NYT charges a mistake for that on #1073.

Today’s Difficulty & Pattern

Overall: medium–hard. Yellow is a clean semantic solve; green is medium because every word has a tempting literal meaning; blue is hard if you did not read middle-grade fiction in the 1970s–80s; purple is hard until you say the board aloud.

Pattern note: #1073 is a homograph festival—success depends on locking one sense per word before you burn guesses on cross-band overlaps (food vs. fraud, clinic vs. crib, fat vs. fiction).
