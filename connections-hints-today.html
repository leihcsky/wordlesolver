Connections Hints Today (Spoiler-Free)

Hint 1 Yellow Category Clue

Four entries share the same spoken sound: a couple, a trim, a fruit, and a French father word with an accent. Spelling is the giveaway—not definition.

How to use it: Say each word aloud; if they rhyme or match, you have yellow. Do not mix in “pair” as in sports teams.

Hint 2 Green Category Clue

Each word can describe something splitting, bursting, or failing structurally—often as a verb or short noun phrase (“___ apart,” “a ___ in the dam”).

How to use it: Ignore weather-only readings of BLOW; test the “break open” sense for all four before you submit.

Hint 3 Blue Category Clue

All four are names fans use for Major League Baseball franchises—think cities and club nicknames, not generic colors or royalty in isolation.

How to use it: PADRE might look like Spanish “father”—here it is a team. RED is not just a color; ROYAL and TWIN are not adjectives alone.

Hint 4 Purple Category Clue

Rearrange the letters in CHEAP, EARP, LUMP, and WIKI and each becomes a fruit. None of the four words are fruits on the board.

How to use it: Save purple for last; pencil anagrams on paper. WIKI and EARP look like tech/history—trust the scramble, not the surface meaning.

How to Use Today’s Hints

  • Yellow first: Read hint 1 or tap the yellow bar—sound-alike sets are usually the cleanest lock for #1072.
  • Green second: Once homophones are gone, test BLOW/CRACK/POP/SPLIT on “break or burst,” not weather or soda alone.
  • Blue when stuck: Hint 3 is trivia-heavy; if you do not follow MLB, peek the blue bar clue before burning a mistake.
  • Purple last: Anagrams punish early guesses—only open hint 4 after twelve words remain and three groups are clear.

Today’s Connections Analysis

Yellow Group Sound-alike spellings

How to identify it: PAIR, PARE, PEAR, and PÈRE are the classic “say them out loud” set. Lock this before you chase fruit-with-pair mashups.

Possible confusion: PEAR looks like it could join a food group with CHEAP or LUMP—if the rule is not identical pronunciation, it is wrong.

Green Group Breaking or bursting

How to identify it: BLOW, CRACK, POP, and SPLIT share rupture verbs—test “something can ___” or “a ___ in the surface.”

Possible confusion: BLOW as wind or POP as soda flavor is a one-word misread; all four must fit the same break-open idea.

Blue Group Baseball franchises

How to identify it: PADRE, RED, ROYAL, and TWIN map to MLB teams (San Diego, Cincinnati/Boston context, Kansas City, Minnesota). Trivia-heavy but tight once you see it.

Possible confusion: RED as a color with ROYAL as “royal blue,” or PADRE as Spanish father—those readings do not include all four words.

Purple Group Fruit anagrams

How to identify it: CHEAP → PEACH, EARP → GRAPE, LUMP → PLUM, WIKI → KIWI. The surface words misdirect—only letter rearrangement unifies them.

Possible confusion: Treating CHEAP as “low cost” or WIKI as websites; purple needs the same transformation rule for every tile.

How to Solve Today’s Puzzle Step by Step

  1. Step 1: Scan for homophones—read PAIR, PARE, PEAR, PÈRE aloud and submit if the sound matches.
  2. Step 2: With twelve words left, try the rupture quartet (BLOW, CRACK, POP, SPLIT) before you spend mistakes on sports.
  3. Step 3: If PADRE and RED remain, ask “MLB team nickname?” for each candidate—not dictionary definitions alone.
  4. Step 4: Anagram the last four on paper; one fruit per word confirms purple without needing the category title early.

Common Mistakes in Today’s Puzzle

  • Putting PEAR with CHEAP/LUMP because they feel “grocery related” instead of checking pronunciation or anagrams.
  • Grouping RED + ROYAL as colors while leaving PADRE and TWIN orphaned.
  • Using BLOW only as weather and dropping CRACK/POP/SPLIT from the same rule.
  • Submitting WIKI with tech words when the purple rule is letter rearrangement into fruit.
  • Revealing topic bars too early—use clues first, full category names only at the bottom reveal.

Today’s Difficulty & Pattern

Overall medium-hard: yellow is a quick win if you say words aloud; green is fair; blue punishes non-baseball fans; purple is classic NYT misdirection (CHEAP/WIKI/EARP). Expect 1–2 mistakes if you solve yellow first and save anagrams for last.
